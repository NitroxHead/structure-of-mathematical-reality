# Chapter 7: Groups: The Language of Symmetry
## Phase III: The Grammar of Structure
### Slides

## Where This Sits

- Chapter 5 gave symmetry as an intuition: transformations that leave something unchanged.
- This chapter turns that word into an object you can compute with.
- A group is the exact formalization of symmetry.
- Payoff: crystals, equations, card shuffles, and conservation laws become one structure.

## The Four Axioms

A group is a set G with an operation · such that:

1. **Closure**: a · b is in G.
2. **Associativity**: (a · b) · c = a · (b · c).
3. **Identity**: an e with e · a = a · e = a.
4. **Inverses**: each a has a⁻¹ with a · a⁻¹ = e.

> Read them as one thing: reversible operations that can be composed.

## What a Group Captures

- The transformations that leave a property unchanged always satisfy the four axioms.
- The axioms are not imposed on symmetry; they are what symmetry automatically is.
- molecule → spectroscopy; equation → solutions; spacetime → the Lorentz group.
- permutations → combinatorics and cryptography; quantum system → particle classes.

## Worked Example: The Square

- D₄ = the 8 rigid motions that leave a square looking identical.
- Four rotations (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) and four reflections.
- Closure, identity (0°), inverses (270° undoes 90°), associativity: all hold.
- Rotate-then-reflect ≠ reflect-then-rotate: groups need not be commutative.

## When It Is Not a Group

- Integers under + : a group (identity 0, inverse of 7 is -7).
- Integers under × : inverses fail (1/7 is not an integer). Not a group.
- Preprocessing transforms: the deciding axiom is always inverses.
- A lossy step (projection, aggregation, clipping) kills invertibility.

> "Can I undo this step?" and "Do my transforms form a group?" are the same question.

## Homomorphisms

- A map f between groups that preserves the operation: f(a · b) = f(a) · f(b).
- Combine-then-map lands where map-then-combine lands.
- This is "same structure, different content" made precise.
- An *isomorphism* is a homomorphism that is also a bijection.

## Isomorphism = Same Group, Different Clothes

- The 4 rotations of a square and {0,1,2,3} under addition mod 4: isomorphic.
- Same structure, so anything true of one is true of the other.
- But same size does not mean same group.

| Order-4 group | Generated by one element | Every element self-inverse |
|---|---|---|
| ℤ₄ (cyclic) | yes | no |
| Klein four | no | yes |

## The Theme That Runs Everywhere

- The right map between structures is the one that preserves the structure.
- Group homomorphism preserves the operation.
- Linear map (Chapter 8) preserves addition and scaling.
- Continuous map (Chapter 9) preserves nearness.
- What you choose to preserve *defines* the field you are in.

## Subgroups: Partial Symmetry

- A subgroup is a group sitting inside a larger group.
- The 4 rotations are a subgroup of the 8 symmetries in D₄.
- They are the symmetries that also preserve handedness (never flip).
- Subgroups name the finer distinctions a system can make.

## Quotients: Coarsen on Purpose

- Declare all elements related by a subgroup to be "the same."
- Integers, collapse multiples of 3 → ℤ/3ℤ, the three-position clock.
- What is discarded: which multiple of 3. What survives: the remainder.
- Subgroup zooms in; quotient zooms out. A resolution control on symmetry.

## Representations

- Abstract group = what symmetries exist and how they compose.
- Representation = how they act, as matrices on a vector space.
- Composition of group elements becomes matrix multiplication.
- This is where group theory meets linear algebra (Chapter 8).

## Why Spectroscopy Can Predict

- Two molecules, different chemistry, same symmetry group.
- Their vibrations form a space the group acts on: a representation.
- It splits into the group's irreducible pieces, fixed by the group alone.
- So the *pattern* of distinct frequencies is set by the group, not the chemistry.
- SO(3) and continuous groups carry Noether: symmetry → conserved quantity.

## The Erlangen Program

- Klein, 1872: a geometry is the study of what a transformation group leaves invariant.
- Rigid motions → distance, angle → Euclidean.
- Add scaling → ratios, parallelism → affine. Projective → straightness, cross-ratio.
- Any continuous deformation → only holes and connectivity → topology (Chapter 9).
- Bigger group → fewer invariants → coarser geometry.

> The field you stand in is set by the transformations you agree to ignore.

## Takeaway and Bridge

- Symmetry is now an algebra: groups, homomorphisms, subgroups, quotients, representations.
- One thesis unifies Chapters 3, 5, and 7: choosing a group is choosing a geometry.
- But representations act on a *vector space*, and we never said what that is.
- Chapter 8: what a canvas is, and why linearity lives in the canvas, not the system.
